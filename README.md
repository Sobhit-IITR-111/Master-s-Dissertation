# Master's Dissertation | Rock Physics & Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)
Topic: "Quantifying Texturally Controlled Physical and Seismic Rock Properties in Pre- and Post-CO₂ Sequestered Basalts"

1) Investigated the impact of CO₂ sequestration on the elastic, attenuation, and microstructural properties of Deccan basalt samples using ultrasonic laboratory measurements and digital rock analysis.
2) Developed Python-based workflows for automated P-, Sv-, and Sh-wave processing, spectral-ratio attenuation analysis (Qp and Qs estimation), interactive arrival picking, and real-time frequency-domain characterization.
3) Quantified pore-scale textural evolution through pore aspect ratio analysis from digital rock volumes and linked microstructural changes to variations in seismic velocities, attenuation coefficients, and quality factors.
4) Established relationships between attenuation attributes (Qp, Qs, Qs/Qp), elastic parameters (Vp, Vs, Vp/Vs, Poisson's ratio), and pore geometry to characterize fluid-rock interactions induced by CO₂ mineralization.
5) Demonstrated the potential of the **Qs/Qp–Vp/Vs state-space framework** as a fluid discriminator for tracking CO₂ saturation and pore-fluid evolution in basaltic reservoirs.
6) Integrated ultrasonic attenuation measurements with digital rock physics observations to develop a laboratory-scale rock physics framework for interpreting time-lapse geophysical responses associated with geological carbon storage.
7) Contributed toward understanding how pore-scale alterations caused by CO₂ sequestration may influence larger-scale seismic observables, supporting future CCS monitoring strategies in basalt formations.

# Summary: I worked on investigating the effectiveness and feasibility of CO2 sequestration in basalts by quantifying the physical the seismic rock properties in Pre and Post CO2 state that will help us to constitute the rock physics framework which builds the gap between micro scale textural properties evolution with the macro-scale time lapse-seismic monitoring of CO2.
